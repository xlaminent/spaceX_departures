reusable launch system

The reusable launch system-program was announced back in 2011 and since has years gone into developing the technology needed to facilitate full and rapid reusability of space launch vehicles.
This program was both developed and used for the first stage of Falcon 9 rockets, but the long term goal was to create a design that would allow both stages of a orbital launch vehicle to be reused just a few hours after return.

A successfull landing and revovery of a first stage was achived in desember 2015. In march of 2017 the first re-flight of a previously landed first stage happened, making way for what soon would become a routine. As of march 2020 individual boosters have powered up to five missions.

New Technologies

Facilitates successful launch and recovery of the first stages of Falcon 9 and Heavy, and both stages of Starship.

Restartable ignition system

At supersonic velocities in the upper atmosphere restarts are required in order to in order to reverse the high velocity and put the booster on a descent trajectory toward the launch pad.

Improved attitude controls

Responsible for bringing the descending rocket through the atmosphere in a manner conducive both to non-destructive return and sufficient aerodynamic control such that the terminal phase of the landing is possible.

Hypersonic grid fins

Made from forged titanium - grid fins enable precision landing. Arranged in an X-configuration they control the descending rockets lift vector once it has returned to the atmosphere to enable a more precise landing location.

Moving Forward

As of 2020 the focus is on the development of the STARSHIP SYSTEM, which is intended to create a reusable two-stage launch vehicle. It will replace existing vehicles used for both cargo delivery and human transport. Eventually it will support flights to the moon and mars, and could be used for point-to-point travel on Earth. Starship will be capable of taking people from any city to any other city on Earth in under one hour.
